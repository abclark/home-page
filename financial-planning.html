Financial Planning in the AI Era

Log of a week spent teaching an AI to manage my money.

Background: I spent three years at Wealthfront building a robo-advisor — a stochastic optimization engine that planned 250,000 users' financial lives. We modeled every account type, tax rule, and contribution limit. Five years later I tried replacing the idea with an AI and a folder of bank statements.

Feb 11

Set up an AI assistant on a Raspberry Pi. Email, calendar, grocery list. The plumbing. What I actually wanted: feed it every account, every paycheck, every tax return, and have it tell me what to do.

Feb 12

Exported 50,000 WhatsApp messages and 4,500 iMessages from SQLite. Not about finance — about building enough context that the advice would be personal, not generic.

Feb 13

Financial data went in. Private GitHub repo with CSVs from Vanguard, Schwab, Chase, Capital One, Venmo. Portfolio positions, transaction histories, RSU vesting schedules.

First pass: net worth ~$1.6M including unvested stock. 70% of liquid assets in three tech stocks. Contributing to a 529 at a rate that assumes children I don't have. Connected a Wahoo bike computer. Set up cron jobs to sync repos daily and update prices weekly.

Feb 14

Drove to Tahoe. In the car, had the AI audit my public profile. It found my 2007 Australian election candidacy (455 votes, age 20), my Harvard dissertation committee, and a ZoomInfo profile with my work email. Started a cleanup.

Also had it draft job search materials — target companies, outreach templates, interview prep. All in files, nothing sent.

Feb 15

Backcountry skiing near Donner Summit. Tested voice dictation to the AI through Telegram while standing in snow. Worked. Added 21 birthdays to Google Calendar.

Feb 16

Forwarded compensation letters, W-2s, tax returns, Social Security statement. The AI extracted every line item and built a dashboard.

Findings:

  • 401k was 56% Roth, 37% pre-tax. Wrong election for years.
  • $10,429 stuck in after-tax 401k — should have been converted to Roth IRA months ago.
  • W-4 set to Single/$0 additional. RSU income withheld at 22% against a 35% marginal rate. Under-withheld ~$15,000/year. Explained the April surprise every year.
  • $562 in credit card interest over two years. Not broke — 401k contributions are front-loaded, creating a winter cash crunch.
  • Unclaimed employer perks: free Goldman Sachs financial consulting, $1,200/year education reimbursement, $17/month ClassPass.

Feb 17

Settled pre-tax vs Roth. The whole debate is one inequality.

You have $1 of pre-tax income. Two options:

  • Pre-tax: $1 × (1+r)n × (1 − τf)
  • Roth: $1 × (1 − τn) × (1+r)n

Growth cancels. Pre-tax wins iff τn > τf. Returns, inflation, time horizon — all irrelevant.

My τn = 46.65%. To find τf: project the pre-tax balance at real return, add Social Security, subtract standard deduction, apply today's brackets. Works exactly because brackets are inflation-indexed. My retirement income lands in the 12% bracket. Gap is 30 points.

Crossover: need $3.1M in pre-tax to make Roth competitive. IRS caps contributions at $24,500/year. Max that for 28 years and you get $1.6M. Half the crossover. The cap makes it impossible to overshoot.

Connected Chase to Plaid — it had blocked every previous attempt. Built a cash-flow monitor that checks balances against upcoming auto-pay dates and alerts me before a payment bounces.
