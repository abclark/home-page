Productivity, prices, and money expansion

The exercise

  • TFP grew ~1.1%/yr postwar U.S.
  • Fixed money supply + stable velocity → more output per dollar → prices fall ~1.1%/yr
  • Actual CPI rose ~3.5%/yr
  • Gap: ~4.6%/yr relative to the fixed-money counterfactual
  • Cumulative over 70 years: large

Assumptions

  • Money supply fixed (it wasn't)
  • Velocity stable (roughly)
  • TFP gains distribute evenly across the consumption basket (they don't — see below)

Concession 1: productivity needn't show up as lower prices

  • Gains can go to wages, profits, or quality improvements at stable prices
  • Pass-through is weak in services, housing, healthcare
  • Stronger in tradables/durables
  • So the aggregate gap overstates the consumer price component

Concession 2: money expansion has winners and losers

  • Winners: debtors, asset holders, first recipients of new money
  • Losers: fixed-income savers, wage earners whose pay adjusts with a lag
  • This is a distributional question, separate from the price-level question
